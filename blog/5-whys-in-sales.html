The Power of Asking "Why" in Sales

Asking "why" at least five times is a technique that helps us get to the heart of the problem. It helps you get to the core of the issue, create a compelling problem statement, and build a strong business case that stakeholders can't ignore.

Here's why this technique is so powerful:

  1. Surface-level problems are often just symptoms. Asking "why" repeatedly lets you peel back the layers and find the real cause.
  2. Understanding the root cause helps you frame the problem in a way that resonates with decision-makers and aligns with their priorities. This is key to getting their buy-in.
  3. When you know the real problem, you can create a targeted, effective solution. This sets you up for success when building your business case and positioning your product as the perfect fit.

Let's see this in action:

  • Prospect: "Our devs are complaining that pull requests take too long to merge."
  • You: "Why are long merge times a concern?" (Why #1)
  • Prospect: "Devs are waiting forever for code changes, slowing down development."
  • You: "Why are merge times so long?" (Why #2)
  • Prospect: "We've got a complex CI/CD pipeline with lots of testing and validation. PRs get stuck waiting for tests."
  • You: "Why do PRs get stuck waiting for tests?" (Why #3)
  • Prospect: "Our test suite is huge and covers tons of edge cases. It's great for quality but makes test runs long."
  • You: "Why does a large test suite impact merge times?" (Why #4)
  • Prospect: "Right now, every PR triggers a full run of the entire test suite, even for small changes."
  • You: "Why is it important to optimize testing and improve merge times?" (Why #5)
  • Prospect: "If we can merge PRs faster without sacrificing quality, our devs can be more productive, avoid delays, and release new features faster."

The "5 Whys" technique uncovers the real problem and aligns your solution with stakeholders' priorities. Digging deeper to find the root cause helps you build a compelling case and close the deal.

But wait, there's more! Uncovering the root cause has other benefits:

  1. Keeping buyers engaged: In complex sales cycles, buyers can get fatigued or distracted. Clearly explaining the root cause and its impact keeps them focused on solving the issue.
  2. Elevating the conversation: A significant root cause can grab the attention of higher-ups. Framing the problem in terms of broader business goals (like boosting developer velocity) shifts the conversation from a technical solution to a strategic initiative.
  3. Quantifying the value: Once you understand the root cause, you can project the potential value of achieving the buyer's goals, like time saved or productivity gained. Attaching tangible value to the solution helps stakeholders see the potential ROI and can speed up decision-making.
  4. Qualifying the opportunity: Some buyers might not be able to answer the questions to find the root cause, or the root cause might be outside the scope of what you can offer. Either way, it's better to know that sooner rather than later. This helps you qualify the opportunity and avoid wasting time on deals that aren't a good fit.

In our example, identifying the inefficient testing strategy as the root cause helps you keep the buyer focused, even if other priorities pop up. You can elevate the conversation from "buying a new testing tool" to "improving developer productivity and delivering features faster." And you can work with the buyer to quantify the potential time savings and productivity gains, showing the real value of your solution.

If the buyer struggles to answer the "why" questions or the root cause turns out to be something you can't address, you can quickly identify that this might not be the right opportunity. This saves you time and resources, allowing you to focus on deals where you can make a real impact.
